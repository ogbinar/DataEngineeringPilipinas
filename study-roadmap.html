Recommended Traditional Degrees

Here are a few recommended degrees that serve as good foundation for these type of work. Note that this is not a critical requirement as anyone can career shift to any role given enough time and upskilling. Please don’t treat this as a restriction but more of a guide on the relevant skillsets or learning outcomes that you would want when taking on these roles.

1. Data Analyst

  1. Bachelor of Science in Statistics - Central to understanding data analysis, this degree equips students with essential skills in data interpretation, probability, and statistical analysis.

  2. Bachelor of Science in Mathematics - Provides comprehensive training in analytical thinking and problem-solving, which are critical for analyzing and deriving insights from data.

  3. Bachelor of Science in Computer Science - Teaches programming, algorithms, and data structures, which are crucial for data manipulation and analysis.

2. Data Engineer

  1. Bachelor of Science in Computer Science - Offers essential knowledge in software development, algorithms, and systems design necessary for building and optimizing data systems.

  2. Bachelor of Science in Electrical Engineering - Includes training in digital systems and circuit design, which can be crucial for understanding the hardware aspect of data processing and storage.

  3. Bachelor of Science in Information Technology - Focuses on database management, system administration, and networking, foundational for maintaining robust data pipelines and architectures.

3. Data Steward

  1. Bachelor of Science in Information Systems - Emphasizes the management of information systems and data governance, which align well with the responsibilities of data stewardship.

  2. Bachelor of Science in Business Administration - With a focus on management information systems, this degree helps in understanding the business implications of data management.

  3. Bachelor of Science in Library Science - Although less common, this degree covers data curation and management, critical for overseeing data lifecycle management and governance.

4. Data Scientist

  1. Bachelor of Science in Statistics - Provides a strong statistical background necessary for data modeling, statistical testing, and data-driven decision-making.

  2. Bachelor of Science in Mathematics - Essential for understanding the underlying algorithms used in data science, including linear algebra and numerical methods.

  3. Bachelor of Science in Computer Science - Helps in mastering the technical and computational skills needed to handle large datasets and perform complex data analysis.

5. AI / ML Engineer

  1. Bachelor of Science in Computer Science - Fundamental for understanding algorithms, machine learning, and software development, which are core to AI/ML engineering.

  2. Bachelor of Science in Mathematics - Key for developing algorithms and models in AI and ML, especially through courses in statistics, probability, and abstract math.

  3. Bachelor of Science in Cognitive Science - Offers interdisciplinary insights into how the human mind works, which can be invaluable in developing AI that mimics human decision-making processes.

Finding Creative Work Experience

For individuals interested in data-related fields, finding the right internships and experience-building opportunities can greatly enhance their career prospects. Here are some creative examples of alternative sources and types of opportunities where data enthusiasts can look to gain relevant experience:

  1. Hackathons and Data Competitions: Participating in hackathons or online data competitions, such as those hosted on Kaggle, DrivenData, or Zindi, can provide hands-on experience with real-world data problems. These platforms often offer challenges sponsored by companies, which can sometimes lead to internships or job offers.

  2. Volunteering for Nonprofits: Many nonprofit organizations need help with data analysis but lack the resources to hire full-time data professionals. Volunteering your skills can be a mutually beneficial arrangement: nonprofits get much-needed data insight, and volunteers gain practical experience.

  3. University Research Projects: Collaborating with professors or joining research projects at universities can offer valuable experience in data handling and analysis. This is especially relevant for students but can also be an option for external collaborators interested in academic research.

  4. Freelance Projects: Websites like Upwork, Freelancer, or Fiverr allow budding data scientists and analysts to take on freelance projects. This can provide varied and practical experience, building a portfolio that can impress future employers.

  5. Open Source Project Contributions: Contributing to open source projects can be a great way to gain experience. Projects related to data science and machine learning often need volunteers to help with data cleaning, analysis, and even model development.

  6. Industry Conferences and Meetups: Attending industry conferences or local meetups can not only provide learning opportunities but also networking prospects. Networking can often lead to internship offers or project collaborations with professionals in the field.

  7. Online Internship Platforms Specific to Tech: Platforms like Internshala, AngelList, or Chegg Internships often list internships specific to tech and data roles. They sometimes feature startup companies offering remote internships, which can provide exposure to fast-paced tech environments.

  8. Government and Public Data Initiatives: Engaging with government open data initiatives or entering challenges hosted by governmental agencies can provide exposure to large-scale data sets and community service.

  9. Corporate Summer Trainee Programs: Some large corporations offer summer trainee programs in data analytics and business intelligence. These are structured to give a deep dive into corporate data handling and can sometimes lead to pre-placement offers.

  10. Social Media Groups and Online Communities: Joining groups on LinkedIn, Reddit (like r/dataisbeautiful), or specific forums can lead to unexpected opportunities such as short-term projects, internships, or learning collaborations.
